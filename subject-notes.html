Percentage, Ratio & Proportion

These three topics together form ~25% of every Quant paper. Master the interplay between them.

Core Percentage Formulas
% of a numberX% of Y = (X × Y) / 100
% change% Change = [(New − Old) / Old] × 100
successive %Net% = a + b + (ab/100)   [use − for decrease]
% to decimalx% = x/100  |  Multiply by the number directly
Ratio & Proportion Rules
  • R1If a:b = 3:5 and b:c = 4:7, then a:b:c = 12:20:35 (multiply to make b equal).
  • R2Proportionality: If a/b = c/d, then ad = bc (cross-multiply). Product of means = product of extremes.
  • R3Componendo-Dividendo: If a/b = c/d, then (a+b)/(a-b) = (c+d)/(c-d). Use to simplify complex ratio equations.
  • R4Partnership Rule: Profit is split in ratio of (Capital × Time). If A invests ₹3000 for 4 months, contribution = 12000 units.
03

Simple & Compound Interest

High-frequency topic. Always check whether CI or SI is asked. Know the shortcut differences.

Simple Interest
SI = (P × R × T) / 100
Amount = P + SI

-- If SI for 2yr = 400,
-- Annual SI = 200
-- P = SI × 100 / (R × T)
Compound Interest
A = P(1 + R/100)ⁿ
CI = A − P

-- Half-yearly: R/2, n×2
-- Quarterly: R/4, n×4
-- Monthly: R/12, n×12
CI vs SI Shortcuts
Diff (2yr) = P(R/100)²
Diff (3yr) = P(R/100)²(3+R/100)

-- CI for 2yr at 10%:
-- = SI + 10% of 1st yr SI
-- CI always > SI for same P,R,T
🎯 Exam Trick — Population/Depreciation Same formula as CI. Population after n years = P(1 + r/100)ⁿ. Depreciation: P(1 − r/100)ⁿ. Price increases by different rates each year: P × (1+r₁/100) × (1+r₂/100) × (1+r₃/100).

Profit & Loss

Read carefully — "marked price", "selling price", and "cost price" are three different things.

Key Formulas
profit %Profit% = (Profit / CP) × 100  |  Loss% = (Loss / CP) × 100
SP from CPSP = CP × (100 + Profit%) / 100  |  SP = CP × (100 − Loss%) / 100
successive discountsNet Discount = a + b − ab/100   (like successive % change)
dishonest shopkeeperProfit% = [(True weight − False weight) / False weight] × 100
⚠️ Common Traps "Profit of 20% on SP" ≠ "Profit of 20% on CP". On CP: profit = 0.2 × CP. On SP: if SP = 100, profit = 20, so CP = 80, which means profit on CP = 25%. Always identify the base!
06

Time & Work

Always solve using the LCM method (Work Units). Faster than fractions every time.

📐 The LCM Work Method (Non-Negotiable) Step 1: Find LCM of all given days — that is your Total Work. Step 2: Efficiency = Total Work / Days. Step 3: Combined efficiency = sum. Step 4: Time = Total Work / Combined Efficiency. Example: A=10 days, B=15 days → LCM=30 units. A does 3/day, B does 2/day. Together: 5/day. Time = 30/5 = 6 days.
Pipes & Cisterns (Same Concept)
  • P1Inlet pipe fills tank. Outlet pipe empties it. If both open: Net rate = Fill rate − Empty rate.
  • P2If inlet fills in 6hrs and outlet empties in 10hrs, net = 1/6 − 1/10 = 2/30 = 1/15. Tank fills in 15hrs.
  • P3If pipe fills 3/4 tank in 9 hours, it fills full tank in 12 hours. Always find unit rate first.

Mixture & Alligation

A visual method that turns complex mixing problems into simple cross-multiplication.

📊 The Alligation Cross Rule Place Cheaper price (C) top-left, Dearer price (D) top-right, Mean price (M) in center.
Cross-subtract: Right quantity = M − C. Left quantity = D − M.
Ratio = (M − C) : (D − M) = Cheaper : Dearer.
Example: Milk & Water
-- Milk at ₹20/L, Water free
-- Mean price ₹15/L
Cross: (15−0):(20−15)
= 15:5 = 3:1
Mix 3 parts milk : 1 part water
Removal & Replacement
Final conc. = P(1 − x/V)ⁿ
P = initial amount
x = removed each time
V = vessel capacity
n = number of times

Permutation, Combination & Probability

P&C feeds directly into probability. Understand the counting principle first.

Permutation (Order matters)
ⁿPr = n! / (n−r)!
Arranging 3 from 5 = ⁵P₃
= 5×4×3 = 60
-- "How many ways to arrange"
Combination (Order doesn't)
ⁿCr = n! / [r! × (n−r)!]
Choosing 3 from 5 = ⁵C₃
= 10
-- "How many ways to select"
Probability
P(E) = Favourable / Total
P(A∪B) = P(A)+P(B)−P(A∩B)
P(A') = 1 − P(A)
-- 0 ≤ P(E) ≤ 1 always
PYQ: A train 150m long is running at 72 km/hr. How much time will it take to cross a pole?
A) 5 seconds
B) 6.5 seconds
C) 7.5 seconds
D) 8 seconds
Answer: C

Explanation:
Speed in m/s = 72 × (5/18) = 20 m/s.
Time = Distance / Speed = 150 / 20 = 7.5 seconds.

01

Syllogism — Set Theory Logic

Never use real-world knowledge. Follow strict logical rules. Use Venn diagrams for all questions.

🔑 The Core Principle Syllogism lives in a closed logical world. "All dogs are cats" is valid here even if absurd in reality. Draw diagrams. Never assume. Every conclusion must be provable from the given statements.
Statement Types & Their Logical Meaning
Statement Venn Meaning Definite Conclusions Possible Conclusions
All A are B A ⊂ B (A inside B) Some B are A ✓ / Some A are B ✓ All B are A (possible)
No A is B A ∩ B = ∅ (no overlap) No B is A ✓ / Some A are not B ✓ —
Some A are B A and B overlap partially Some B are A ✓ All A are B / No A is B
Some A are not B Part of A is outside B — All A are B (not possible)
Combination Rules (Chain Syllogism)
  • C1All + All = All: "All A→B" + "All B→C" = "All A→C". A⊂B and B⊂C means A⊂C.
  • C2All + No = No: "All A→B" + "No B→C" = "No A→C". If A is inside B, and B has no overlap with C, then A has no overlap with C.
  • C3Some + All = Some: "Some A→B" + "All B→C" = "Some A→C".
  • C4Some + No = Some Not: "Some A→B" + "No B→C" = "Some A are not C".
  • C5All + Some = No Definite: "All A→B" + "Some B→C" — you CANNOT conclude anything definite about A and C.

Direction & Distance Sense

Draw. Always draw. Mark starting point as origin (0,0) and track each movement on a coordinate grid.

Compass & Shadow Rules
  • D1Facing North, turn Right (CW) 90°: Now facing East. Turn Right again: South. Turn Right again: West.
  • D2Facing North, turn Left (ACW) 90°: Now facing West. Left again: South. Left again: East.
  • D3Morning Shadow (sunrise in East): Shadow always falls to the West (behind you if you face East).
  • D4Evening Shadow (sunset in West): Shadow always falls to the East.
  • D5Distance formula: For right-angle paths, use Pythagoras: D = √(horizontal² + vertical²).

Rule: When Chain Breaks, Conclusion Fails A > B ≥ C → A > C ✓ (chain holds). But A > B and C > B → cannot compare A and C (no chain between them). Always trace the chain from left to right.
PYQ: If 'A + B' means 'A is brother of B', 'A × B' means 'A is father of B'. What does 'P + Q × R' mean?
A) P is the uncle of R
B) P is the father of R
C) P is the brother of R
D) R is the uncle of P
Answer: A

Explanation:
Q × R → Q is father of R.
P + Q → P is brother of Q.
So, P is the brother of R's father, making P the uncle of R.

02

Subject-Verb Agreement (SVA)

The most tested grammar rule. 40% of error spotting questions involve SVA violations.

Core SVA Rules
  • S1Basic Rule: Singular subject → singular verb. Plural subject → plural verb. "He plays" / "They play".
  • S2Either…Or / Neither…Nor: Verb agrees with the subject closest to the verb. "Neither the boys nor the man is coming." (man is singular).
  • S3Collective Nouns (team, jury, committee, class): Singular verb if they act as one unit. Plural if members act individually. "The team is ready." / "The jury are divided."
  • S4Each / Every / Someone / Nobody / Either / Neither: Always take singular verb. "Everyone is here." "Each of the students has a book."
  • S5None: Can be singular or plural depending on context. "None of the water is clean." / "None of the students are ready."
  • S6There is / There are: Verb depends on the noun that follows. "There is a cat." / "There are two cats."
  • S7Mathematical expressions: Usually singular. "Five and five is ten." "Twenty rupees is the price."

Conditionals (If-Then Clauses)

A classic source of Fill-in-the-blanks and Error Spotting. Four types — each with fixed structure.

Type Condition If Clause Main Clause Usage
Type 0 Always true Simple Present Simple Present Ice melts if you heat it.
Type 1 Real/Possible Simple Present Will + V1 If it rains, I will stay home.
Type 2 Hypothetical Simple Past Would + V1 If I were rich, I would travel.
Type 3 Impossible (past) Past Perfect Would have + V3 If she had studied, she would have passed.
⚠️ Classic Error — Type 2 "If I was rich" is WRONG. Always use "If I were rich" — subjunctive mood requires 'were' for all persons (I/he/she/it) in Type 2 conditionals.
04

Articles — A / An / The

Simple rules but commonly tested. Articles wrong = error spotting mark lost.

  • A1A: Before consonant sound. "A university" (yu- sound), "a European" (yu- sound). Sound, not spelling.
  • A2An: Before vowel sound. "An hour" (silent h), "an honest man", "an MBA" (em-sound).
  • A3The: Before specific/known noun. "The moon, the sun, the Earth." Superlatives: "the best, the tallest". Unique things: "the Prime Minister."
  • A4No Article: Before plural general nouns ("Dogs are loyal"), languages ("She speaks French"), meals ("Have breakfast"), sports ("He plays cricket").

Reading Comprehension — Strategy

RC is not about reading fast. It's about reading smart. These strategies cut average time by 40%.

  • RC1Read questions first (not answers). Know what to look for before you enter the passage. Mark keywords in questions.
  • RC2Skim the passage: First sentence of every paragraph = topic sentence. Read these, skip the middle, read conclusion. Build a mental map.
  • RC3Inference questions: Answer must be supported by the passage, not your general knowledge. If the passage doesn't say it, the answer is wrong — even if it's true in real life.
  • RC4Tone questions: Look for adjectives and adverbs. "Unfortunately" = negative tone. "Remarkably" = positive. Author tone ≠ topic tone.
  • RC5Eliminate extremes: Options with "always", "never", "completely", "all" are almost always wrong. Passages rarely make absolute claims.
  • RC6"Best title" questions: Title must capture the MAIN idea, not a detail. If the passage is about XYZ and only one paragraph mentions ABC, don't pick ABC as the title.

PYQ: Spot the error: "Neither the manager nor the employees was aware of the new policy."
A) Neither the manager
B) nor the employees
C) was aware of
D) the new policy.
Answer: C

Explanation:
When subjects are joined by "neither...nor", the verb agrees with the subject closest to it. "Employees" is plural, so it should be "were aware of".
